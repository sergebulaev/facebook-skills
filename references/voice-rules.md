# Voice Rules for Facebook Pages

These are the canonical voice rules for the whole bundle. Every skill inherits
them. Skill-local "Hard rules" sections only add format-specific overrides
(char ranges, the under-80 sweet spot, comment rules) and point back here.

## Hard rules

1. **No em dashes** (`—`), en dashes (`–`), or double dashes (`--`). Biggest AI
   tell in 2026.
2. **Use `..` as a soft pause** when you would reach for an em dash. Reads human
   and matches how people actually type.
3. **Capitalize personal names, company names, product names** (Stripe, Canva,
   Shopify). Lowercase a brand name and it reads as careless.
4. **Write for a Page, not a person.** A Facebook Page is a business voice. It
   can be warm and human, but it is not a personal profile and not a faceless
   corporate account. Talk like a real person who works there.
5. **Specific numbers beat adjectives.** 2.4x beats "way better". $40 beats
   "affordable". One real number where the claim allows it.
6. **One idea per post.** A Page post that tries to say three things gets shared
   zero times. Pick the single point and land it.
7. **Lead short.** The under-80-character post is the highest-engagement shape on
   a Page. Reach for it first; only go long when the story genuinely needs room.

## Vocabulary blacklist

Never use:
- leverage, utilize, facilitate, streamline, robust, seamless, delve, navigate,
  unlock, harness, foster, cultivate
- fundamentally, essentially, ultimately, crucially, notably
- landscape, ecosystem, paradigm, realm, tapestry, journey
- "It's not just X, it's Y"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "game-changer", "deep dive", "at the end of the day", "needle-mover"
- "We are thrilled to announce" (the deadest Page-post opener there is)

## Facebook-Page-native style

- **Short is the native register.** Posts under 80 characters get a reported
  ~66% engagement lift. A Page that posts tight, punchy one-liners outperforms
  one that posts paragraphs, most of the time.
- **Conversational, not corporate.** Ask a real question, share a real moment,
  state a real opinion. The Page posts that travel sound like a person, not a
  press release.
- **Emoji: 0-2, and only when they earn their place.** Facebook audiences
  tolerate slightly more emoji than X or LinkedIn, but a post sprinkled with 6
  reads as spam. A single well-placed emoji can lift a short post.
- **Hashtags barely help on Facebook.** 0-2 max. They are not a discovery engine
  on Pages the way they are on Instagram. Skip them unless one is genuinely a
  campaign tag.
- **Links: expect a preview.** If your post contains a URL, Facebook auto-builds
  a link preview card. Write the post text to stand on its own above that card;
  do not paste a raw link with no framing.

## Length

- **Sweet spot: under 80 characters.** This is the lead shape. One sharp line, a
  real question, a single number, a short reaction.
- **Story posts: keep them under ~500 characters of real substance** even though
  Facebook allows up to 63,206. A long post is a deliberate choice for a story,
  an announcement with stakes, or a behind-the-scenes. It is not the default.
- **The first line is the whole funnel on mobile.** Facebook truncates longer
  posts with a "See more" fold. Everything that earns the tap has to be above
  it, in the first line or two.

## Structure

- **Open with the point, not the windup.** "We shipped it." beats "After months
  of hard work by our incredible team, we are excited to share that..".
- **End on a landing or a real question.** A specific question ("which one would
  you ship first?") earns comments. "What do you think?" is dead.
- **Make sharing easy.** The most shareable Page posts are quotable: a clean
  opinion, a useful tip, a relatable line, or a number worth passing on. Shares
  are the reach multiplier on Facebook, so design for them.

## Anti-patterns

- "We are thrilled / excited / delighted to announce.." openers.
- Generic corporate hype with no specific detail.
- Walls of text with no line breaks.
- Rule of three ("faster, cheaper, better").
- Stuffing 5+ hashtags at the bottom.
- Begging for engagement ("LIKE and SHARE if you agree", "comment YES").
- ALL CAPS for intensity. Carry intensity with word choice.

## Algorithmic note (meaningful interactions)

Facebook's ranking rewards **meaningful interactions**: comments and shares far
outweigh reactions. A reaction is a light tap; a comment is a conversation and a
share re-injects the post into a whole new network. Before posting, check: does
this give a reader a reason to comment or share, not just react? If it only
earns a passive Like, sharpen it. Begging for engagement is downranked, so earn
the interaction with the post itself.
